[ 02. TECHNICAL LEXICON ]

Mechanical
Theory.

Composition
& Framing.

Rule of Thirds
Dividing your frame into a 3x3 grid and placing your subject on the grid lines or intersections. This creates asymmetrical balance and tension, moving the subject away from a stagnant center.
APPLICATION: General portraiture, landscapes.
Golden Triangle
A compositional rule dividing the frame using a diagonal line from corner to corner, with two intersecting perpendicular lines from the remaining corners. Subjects are placed on these dramatic diagonals.
APPLICATION: High-energy action, architecture.
Leading Lines
Linear elements (roads, paths, fences, shadows) that naturally guide the viewer's eyes directly toward the main subject or the vanishing point.
Negative Space
The empty or quiet area surrounding your main subject. Heavy negative space isolates the subject completely, defining scale and isolation.

Lenses
& Optics.

Focal Length: 24mm - 35mm
Wide Angle. Expands the field of view, pulling the environment into the shot. Causes slight edge distortion. Ideal for photojournalism and environmental storytelling where context is king.
EFFECT: Expands background distance.
Focal Length: 50mm
Standard Lens (The Nifty Fifty). Closely mimics human vision with zero distortion. The perfect midpoint between wide-angle context and telephoto compression.
EFFECT: Natural perspective.
Focal Length: 85mm - 200mm
Telephoto. Magnifies distant subjects. More importantly, it creates "Lens Compression," pulling the background violently forward, making distant mountains or cityscapes appear immediately behind the subject.
EFFECT: Flattens features, isolates subject.
Chromatic Aberration
Also known as color fringing; purple or green halos that appear along high-contrast edges caused by a lens failing to focus all colors to the exact same focal point.

Exposure &
Dynamic Range.

Aperture (f-stop)
The mechanical iris inside the lens. A low number (f/1.4) opens the iris wide, letting in massive light and creating a blurry background (Shallow Depth of Field). A high number (f/16) restricts light but keeps everything sharp.
Shutter Speed
The duration the sensor is exposed to light. Fast speeds (1/2000s) mathematically freeze motion like race cars. Slow speeds (1/2s) blur water into a milky texture or create light trails.
ISO Sensitivity
The electronic amplification of the light hitting the sensor. While higher ISOs allow shooting in the dark, they introduce digital noise/grain, degrading the image's structural integrity.
ETTR (Exposing to the Right)
A highly technical digital exposure strategy where the histogram is pushed as far right (bright) as possible without blowing out the highlights. This ensures the shadows have maximum data with minimum noise when darkened in post.

Color Theory &
Processing.

White Balance (Kelvin)
The recalibration of color temperature. Daylight sits around 5500K. If you shoot under warm indoor tungsten bulbs (3200K), your camera must cool the image down by adding blue to achieve true white.
RAW Format
An unprocessed, uncompressed data dump from the sensor. It looks flat out of the camera but contains millions of hidden colors and exposure data points ready to be extracted during editing.
Frequency Separation
An advanced high-end retouching technique (mostly used in fashion/beauty) that separates an image into two distinct layers: Texture (skin pores) and Tone (color/shadows). This allows editors to fix skin blemishes without destroying the natural skin texture.
Dodge and Burn
A darkroom technique brought to digital. Dodging lightens specific areas of an image (like drawing attention to eyes), while Burning darkens areas (like adding deep contrast to cheekbones or shadows).
